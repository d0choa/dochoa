Incorporating information on predicted solvent accessibility to the co-evolution-based study of protein interactions

Mirrortree and related methodologies have been widely used to infer protein-protein interactions and functional relationships from phyogenetic trees. These methods assume that related proteins evolve in a coordinated fashion printing this signal in the topology and distances of their phylogenetic trees. Although some authors showed that co-evolution is more evident when evaluated on the surfaces/interfaces of the proteins, the lack of experimental 3D structures precludes taking advantage of this for improving the performance of the method in general.

To address this limitation, we benchmarked the impact of restricting mirrortree-like analysis to the residues predicted as accessible. In order to do that, we predicted solvent accessibility for each E. coli protein using multiple sequence alignments containing homologous sequences. The trees of orthologous which are the basic input of mirrortree-like methods where then constructed but using only the positions fulfilling certain criteria of predicted accessibility. The global performances obtained by 3 different methods (MirrorTree (MT), Profile Correlation (PC) and ContextMirror (CM)) when fed with these trees were evaluated using 3 datasets representing different types of interactions: binary physical, co-presence in macromolecular complexes and co-membership in the same metabolic pathway.

We found that the performances of the context-based methodologies (PC and CM) in predicting binary-physical interactions are increased when using trees based on predicted accessible positions. Moreover, the methods showed better performance when using alignments constructed ad-hoc for predicting accessibility (i.e. including paralogs) than when using the same alignments of orthologs used for deriving the trees.

The general conclusion is that predicted accessibility only helps in the prediction of protein interactions under some circumstances. The accurate context-based methods predicting binary physical interactions is the ideal case to think about excluding (predicted) not-accessible regions. Moreover, that improvement comes at no cost in terms of applicability since accessibility can be predicted for any sequence.
